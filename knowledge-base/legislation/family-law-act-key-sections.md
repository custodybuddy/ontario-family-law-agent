# Family Law Act (FLA) - Key Sections for Ontario Family Law

**Last Updated:** April 19, 2026  
**Jurisdiction:** Ontario  
**Statute:** Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3  
**Current Version:** As amended

---

## 📋 Overview

The Family Law Act (FLA) is Ontario legislation governing:
- **Property division** between married spouses
- **Spousal support** obligations  
- **Domestic contracts** (marriage contracts, cohabitation agreements, separation agreements)
- **Restraining orders** for family members

**Important:** The FLA applies to **married spouses** only for property division. Common-law partners do NOT have automatic property rights under the FLA.

**Key Concepts:**
- Equalization of Net Family Property (not automatic 50/50 split)
- Matrimonial Home (special treatment)
- Spousal support (separate from property)
- Domestic contracts (enforceable agreements)

---

## 🎯 Part I - Family Property

### **Section 4 - Net Family Property (NFP)**

**Definition:**
Value of ALL property owned on **valuation date**, MINUS value owned on **marriage date**, MINUS debts and liabilities.

**Formula:**
```
NFP = (Property on separation) - (Property on marriage) - (Debts) - (Excluded property)
```

**Valuation date** = Earliest of:
- Date separation with no reasonable prospect of reconciliation
- Date divorce granted
- Date marriage declared null
- Date one spouse dies

**Why NFP matters:** This is what gets equalized, not the actual assets. Each spouse keeps their own property but pays equalizing amount.

---

### **Section 4(2) - Excluded Property**

**Property NOT included in NFP:**

**(a) Gifts or inheritances** from third parties received DURING marriage:
- Inheritance from parents
- Gift from grandparents
- But NOT gifts between spouses

**(b) Income from excluded property** IF the parties agree to exclude it:
- Rental income from inherited property (if agreed)
- Investment returns from gift money (if agreed)

**(c) Damages/settlement for personal injury:**
- Motor vehicle accident settlement
- Medical malpractice award
- But NOT lost wages component

**(d) Life insurance proceeds** received AFTER marriage:
- If spouse was beneficiary
- Amount received during marriage is excluded

**(e) Property excluded by domestic contract:**
- Marriage contract provision
- Separation agreement clause

**(f) Property traced to excluded property:**
- Sold inherited house, bought new property with proceeds
- Must be able to trace connection

**CRITICAL EXCEPTION:**
The **matrimonial home is NEVER excluded**, even if:
- Owned before marriage
- Inherited during marriage
- Received as gift
- Paid for with excluded funds

---

### **Section 5 - Equalization of Net Family Properties**

**(1) Equal sharing principle:**
When marriage ends, spouse with higher NFP pays half the difference to spouse with lower NFP.

**Formula:**
```
Equalization payment = (Higher NFP - Lower NFP) ÷ 2
```

**Example:**
Spouse A NFP: $500,000
Spouse B NFP: $200,000
Difference: $300,000
Equalization: $150,000

**Spouse A pays Spouse B $150,000**

**(2) Payment options:**
- Lump sum
- Installments
- Transfer of property
- By agreement of parties

**Important:** This is NOT a 50/50 split of assets. Each spouse keeps their own property, but pays to equalize the NET growth during marriage.

---

### **Section 5(6) - Deductions and Exclusions**

Can deduct from property value on separation:
- Debts and liabilities
- Value of property brought into marriage
- Excluded property (s.4(2))

Cannot deduct:
- Matrimonial home value (even if owned before marriage)

---

### **Section 6 - Debts**

**(1) Both spouses liable** for debts incurred for:
- Family necessities
- Shelter
- Food, clothing, medical care
- Education

**Even if:**
- Only one spouse incurred debt
- Other spouse didn't consent
- Other spouse didn't benefit

**Exception:** Not liable if creditor knew or should have known spouse lacked authority to contract.

---

### **Section 7 - Equalization Lump Sum**

**(1) Right to half the difference:**
Each spouse entitled to equal share of increase in wealth during marriage.

**(2) Unconscionability:**
Court may award less than half if equal division would be **unconscionable** (shockingly unfair).

---

### **Section 7(3) - Unequal Division (Unconscionability)**

Court may order **unequal division** considering:

**a) Failure to disclose debts/liabilities:**
- Hid credit card debt
- Concealed business losses

**b) Debts or liabilities incurred recklessly:**
- Gambling debts
- Speculative investments
- Drug/alcohol abuse expenses

**c) Intentional depletion of property:**
- Transferred assets to relatives before separation
- Spent down joint accounts on personal items
- Deliberately reduced asset value

**d) Property brought into marriage or inherited:**
- Spouse contributed significantly to preservation/accumulation
- Disproportionate contribution to family wealth

**e) Short marriage duration:**
- Very brief marriage (under 2-3 years)
- Little accumulation of assets
- Equal division seems unfair given short duration

**f) Agreement or court order to support other spouse:**
- Paying support to previous spouse
- Obligation affects ability to pay equalization

**g) Bad faith related to property:**
- Hiding assets
- Fraudulent transfers
- Lying about ownership

**Threshold:** "Unconscionable" = shockingly unfair, beyond just "unfair." High bar to meet.

---

### **Section 10 - Matrimonial Home**

**Definition:**
Property in which person has interest AND is/was ordinarily occupied by person and spouse as family residence.

**Can have multiple matrimonial homes:**
- Cottage used regularly
- City condo and country home

**Special rules:**

**(1) Both spouses have equal right to possession:**
- Even if only one spouse owns it
- Even if owned before marriage
- Cannot be forced out without court order

**(2) Cannot sell without other spouse's consent:**
- Even if only one name on title
- Even if other spouse has no ownership interest
- Protects right to shelter

**(3) NOT excluded from NFP:**
- Even if inherited
- Even if owned before marriage
- Even if received as gift
- Full value included in NFP calculation

**Why special treatment:** Protects the family's shelter, recognized as fundamental need.

---

### **Section 11 - Designation of Matrimonial Home**

**(1) Both must consent:**
To designate property as matrimonial home (for multiple homes).

**(2) Election between homes:**
If multiple matrimonial homes, spouses can agree which one for possession purposes.

---

### **Section 19 - Matrimonial Home Possession**

Court may grant **exclusive possession** to one spouse if:
- Necessary to protect spouse or children
- Would be in best interests
- Financial arrangements can be made

**Factors:**
- Best interests of children
- Existing orders
- Financial position of spouses
- Availability of other accommodation
- Violence or safety concerns

---

### **Section 21 - Partition and Sale**

**(1) Court may order:**
- Partition (division) of property
- Sale of property
- Payment instead of partition

**(2) Considerations:**
- Children's best interests
- Intentions of parties
- Duration of ownership
- Hardship

## 🎯 Part III - Support Obligations

### **Section 30 - Entitlement to Spousal Support**

Every spouse has obligation to provide support for self and other spouse according to need, to the extent capable.

**Entitlement arises from:**
- Marriage relationship
- Economic advantages/disadvantages from marriage
- Economic hardship from marriage breakdown
- Childcare responsibilities

---

### **Section 31 - Effect of Divorce Proceeding**

If divorce proceeding commenced:
- FLA spousal support application stays (pauses)
- Support dealt with under Divorce Act

**Practical:** Usually seek spousal support under Divorce Act if divorcing, FLA if only separated.

---

### **Section 33 - Powers of Court (Spousal Support)**

**(8) Factors to consider:**

**a) Assets and means:**
Current financial position of each spouse.

**b) Capacity to earn:**
- Education, training, experience
- Work history
- Ability to obtain employment
- Time out of workforce

**c) Contribution to each other's education/career:**
- Supported spouse through school
- Sacrificed own career for other's advancement
- Enabled other to pursue opportunities

**d) Needs:**
- Reasonable expenses
- Standard of living during marriage
- Basic needs vs. lifestyle

**e) Obligations to support another person:**
- Children from this or other relationships
- Aging parents

**f) Measures for economic self-sufficiency:**
- Retraining programs
- Education plans
- Employment prospects
- Timeline to independence

**g) Any other relevant matter:**
- Age and health
- Length of marriage
- Functions performed during marriage
- Effect of child-care responsibilities

---

### **Section 34 - Effect of Domestic Contract**

Court can set aside spousal support provisions in domestic contract if:
- Person receiving support failed to disclose significant debts/assets
- Person did not understand nature/consequences of contract
- Otherwise in accordance with law of contract

---

### **Section 37 - Variation**

Court may vary or suspend support order if:
- Material change in circumstances of parties
- Evidence not available at trial

---

## 🎯 Part IV - Domestic Contracts

### **Section 52 - Marriage Contracts**

**(1) Two persons who are married or intend to marry may enter marriage contract.**

**(2) Can contract on:**
- Rights and obligations during marriage or separation
- Ownership/division of property
- Support obligations
- Right to direct education/moral training of children (subject to best interests)
- Any other matter in settlement of affairs

**(3) Cannot contract on:**
- Custody or decision-making (subject to court's best interests jurisdiction)
- Child support (subject to guidelines)

---

### **Section 53 - Cohabitation Agreements**

**(1) Two persons who are cohabiting or intend to cohabit** may enter cohabitation agreement.

**(2) Same provisions as marriage contract.**

**Important:** Common-law partners can contract on property division (since FLA doesn't give them automatic rights).

---

### **Section 54 - Separation Agreements**

**(1) Two persons who cohabited and are living separate and apart** may enter separation agreement.

**(2) Can contract on:**
- Ownership/division of property
- Support obligations
- Right to direct education/moral training of children
- Custody and decision-making
- Any other matter in settlement of affairs

**Note:** Unlike marriage contracts, separation agreements CAN include custody/access provisions.

---

### **Section 56 - Formalities**

**(1) Contract must be:**
- In writing
- Signed by parties
- Witnessed

**(4) Witnessed by:**
- Separate witnesses for each party
- Not party to contract
- Competent to witness

**Why formal requirements:** Ensures parties understand and intend agreement, harder to challenge later.

---

### **Section 59 - Restrictions on Content**

**Cannot include provisions:**
- Purporting to limit person's right to custody/access based solely on sex
- Requiring parties to live in same household
- Requiring sexual relations

---

### **Section 60 - Rights of Donors of Gifts**

Person who gives gift to spouse for purpose of home/household can apply to court if gift used for other purpose.

---

## 🎯 Part V - Dependents' Claims for Damages

### **Section 61 - Right of Dependents to Sue**

Allows spouses and children to claim for loss of guidance, care and companionship if family member injured or killed due to another's fault.

## 🚨 Common Scenarios & Issues

### **Scenario 1: One Spouse Owned Home Before Marriage**

**Facts:**
- Wife owned $400,000 home before marriage
- Home worth $600,000 on separation
- Husband owned nothing on marriage

**Property division:**

Wife's NFP:
- Separation assets: $600,000 (home)
- Marriage date assets: $400,000 (home)
- Matrimonial home NOT excluded
- **NFP = $200,000**

Husband's NFP:
- Separation assets: $50,000 (savings)
- Marriage date assets: $0
- **NFP = $50,000**

**Equalization:**
($200,000 - $50,000) ÷ 2 = $75,000

**Husband gets $75,000 equalization payment**

Even though wife owned home before marriage, matrimonial home is NOT excluded.

---

### **Scenario 2: Inheritance During Marriage**

**Facts:**
- Husband inherited $200,000 during marriage
- Kept separate, not used for matrimonial home
- Can trace to current investment account

**Result:**
- Inheritance is **excluded property**
- Not included in NFP calculation
- Husband keeps full amount

**BUT if:**
- Used inheritance as down payment on matrimonial home
- THEN it's NOT excluded (matrimonial home rule)

---

### **Scenario 3: Business Owner Spouse**

**Facts:**
- Wife owns business
- Started before marriage, worth $100,000
- On separation, worth $500,000
- Husband contributed nothing to business

**Property division:**

Wife's NFP includes:
- Business value on separation: $500,000
- MINUS business value on marriage: $100,000
- Growth during marriage: $400,000 (included in NFP)

**Even though husband didn't work in business, entitled to half the growth during marriage.**

## 💡 Practice Tips

**For Property Division:**

1. **Date everything:**
- Marriage date values crucial
- Keep records of inheritances/gifts
- Document excluded property

2. **Trace excluded property:**
- Keep paper trail
- Don't commingle with marital funds
- Maintain separate accounts

3. **Matrimonial home is special:**
- Can't exclude it even if inherited
- Need consent to sell
- Both have right to possession

4. **Get valuations:**
- Real estate appraisals
- Business valuations
- Pension actuarial reports

5. **Disclose fully:**
- All assets and debts
- Tax returns, bank statements
- Hiding assets = serious consequences

**For Spousal Support:**

1. **Consider both compensatory and non-compensatory:**
- Economic disadvantage from marriage
- Childcare contributions
- Career sacrifices
- Basic needs

2. **Use Spousal Support Advisory Guidelines (SSAG):**
- Not law but widely used
- Provides range of amounts and duration
- Based on length of marriage, incomes

3. **Document contributions:**
- Supported spouse through school
- Primary caregiver for children
- Left workforce for family

4. **Plan for self-sufficiency:**
- Retraining plans
- Employment search efforts
- Timeline to independence
