# Divorce Act - Key Sections for Ontario Family Law

**Last Updated:** April 19, 2026  
**Jurisdiction:** Federal (Applies in Ontario)  
**Statute:** Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.)  
**Current Version:** As amended March 1, 2021

---

## 📋 Overview

The Divorce Act is federal legislation that governs divorce, parenting arrangements, and support for married couples in Canada, including Ontario. The 2021 amendments introduced significant changes to terminology and approach.

**Key Change (March 2021):**
- **OLD:** "Custody" and "Access"
- **NEW:** "Parenting time" and "Decision-making responsibility"

**Why this matters:** The terminology shift reflects a child-focused approach and reduces adversarial language.

---

## 🎯 Core Sections

### **Section 2 - Definitions**

**"Parenting order"**
An order made under this Act that allocates parenting time or decision-making responsibility.

**"Parenting time"**
The time that a child of the marriage spends in the care of a person, whether or not the child is physically with that person during that entire time.

**"Decision-making responsibility"**
The responsibility for making significant decisions about a child's wellbeing, including decisions regarding:
- Health
- Education  
- Culture, language, religion and spirituality
- Significant extra-curricular activities

**"Contact order"**
An order under this Act that provides for a person (other than a parent) to have contact with a child.

**"Family violence"**
Any conduct, whether or not it constitutes a criminal offence, by a family member towards another family member, that is violent or threatening or that constitutes a pattern of coercive and controlling behaviour or that causes that other family member to fear for their own safety or for that of another person.

Includes:
- Physical abuse (hitting, slapping, pushing)
- Sexual abuse
- Threats to kill or cause bodily harm
- Harassment (including stalking)
- Failure to provide necessities of life
- Psychological abuse
- Financial abuse
- Threats to kill or harm an animal or damage property
- Killing or harming an animal or damaging property
- Any pattern of coercive and controlling behaviour
- Exposure of the child to family violence

## 👶 Section 16 - Best Interests of the Child

**Section 16(1) - Paramount Consideration**

The court shall take into consideration only the best interests of the child of the marriage in making a parenting order.

**Section 16(2) - Parenting Order**

The court may make a parenting order that:
- Allocates parenting time
- Allocates decision-making responsibility
- Appoints a person to assist in resolving disputes

**Section 16(3) - Primary Considerations - Child's Best Interests**

In determining the best interests of a child, the court shall consider all factors related to the circumstances of the child, including:

**(a) Child's needs:**
- Physical, emotional and psychological safety, security and wellbeing

**(b) Developmental stage:**
- Stage of development, such as age and maturity of the child

**(c) Meaningful relationships:**
- Nature and strength of child's relationship with:
  - Each parent
  - Each sibling
  - Each grandparent
  - Any other significant person

**(d) Cultural connections:**
- Child's cultural, linguistic, religious and spiritual upbringing and heritage, including Indigenous upbringing and heritage

**(e) Child's views:**
- Child's views and preferences, giving due weight to the child's age and maturity (unless they cannot be ascertained)

**(f) Plans for child's care:**
- Each parent's plan for the child's care

**(g) Ability to care:**
- Ability and willingness to:
  - Care for and meet the needs of the child
  - Communicate and cooperate on matters affecting the child

**(h) Family violence:**
- Any family violence and its impact on:
  - Child's safety, security and wellbeing
  - The ability to care for the child

**(i) Civil or criminal proceedings:**
- Any civil or criminal proceeding, order, condition or measure that is relevant to the safety, security and wellbeing of the child

**(j) Ability to provide:**
- Each parent's ability to exercise parenting time and decision-making responsibility
- Willingness to support child's relationship with other parent

**Section 16(4) - Past Conduct**

In determining the best interests of a child, the court shall not consider the past conduct of any person unless the conduct is relevant to the ability of that person to act as a parent OR the conduct involved family violence.

**What this means:**
- Old relationship conflicts don't matter
- Who filed for divorce doesn't matter
- Who had an affair doesn't matter
- UNLESS it relates to parenting ability or involved family violence

**Section 16(5) - Most Important Factors**

In determining the best interests of the child, the court shall give primary consideration to:
- The child's physical, emotional and psychological safety, security and wellbeing
- Appropriate arrangements can be made to protect the child

**Section 16(6) - Additional Factors**

The court may also consider:
- Any other relevant factor

## 👨‍👩‍👧 Section 16.1 - Family Violence Considerations

**Section 16.1(1) - Presence of family violence**

In considering the impact of family violence, the court shall take into account:

**(a) Nature, seriousness and frequency** of the family violence

**(b) Whether there is a pattern** of coercive and controlling behaviour

**(c) Whether the family violence is directed toward the child** or whether the child is directly or indirectly exposed to the family violence

**(d) Physical, emotional and psychological harm** to the child

**(e) Any compromise to the safety** of the child or other family member

**(f) Whether the family violence causes the child or other family member to fear for their own safety** or for the safety of another person

**(g) Steps taken by the person** engaging in the family violence to prevent further family violence from occurring and improve their ability to care for the child

**(h) Any other relevant factor**

**Section 16.1(2) - Impact on ability to care**

When considering a parent's ability to care for and meet the needs of the child, the court shall consider any impact of family violence on:
- The parent's or child's ability to care for the child
- The person's ability to communicate and cooperate on matters affecting the child

**Section 16.1(3) - Nature of contact**

When considering family violence, if the court determines it would be appropriate to make a parenting order, the court shall consider:
- Whether any terms or conditions should be imposed
- Whether parenting time or decision-making responsibility should be allocated exclusively to one person

## 📏 Section 16.2 - Past Conduct

The court shall not consider the past conduct of any person unless:

**(a)** The conduct is relevant to the exercise of their parenting time, decision-making responsibility or contact with the child under a contact order, OR

**(b)** The conduct constitutes family violence

**Examples:**

✅ **Relevant past conduct:**
- Pattern of not following through on commitments to child
- History of domestic violence
- Substance abuse affecting parenting
- Mental health issues affecting ability to care for child
- Criminal conduct affecting safety

❌ **Irrelevant past conduct:**
- Who initiated the separation
- Extramarital affairs (unless affected parenting)
- Financial disputes (unless relevant to child support)
- General relationship conflicts

## 🤝 Section 16.3 - Maximum Contact Principle

**Section 16.3**

In allocating parenting time, the court shall give effect to the principle that a child should have as much time with each parent as is consistent with the best interests of the child.

**Critical Understanding:**

**"Maximum contact" does NOT mean:**
- ❌ Equal time is required
- ❌ 50/50 is the starting point
- ❌ Both parents get the same amount of time

**"Maximum contact" DOES mean:**
- ✅ The amount of time that serves THIS child's best interests
- ✅ As much time as is beneficial (not harmful) to the child
- ✅ Maintaining meaningful relationships with both parents
- ✅ Could be 50/50, 70/30, 80/20, or any arrangement

**Court considerations for parenting time:**
- Child's developmental needs
- Each parent's availability
- Distance between homes
- School and activity schedules
- Ability of parents to communicate
- History of care
- Any safety concerns

## 🔄 Section 17 - Variation, Rescission or Suspension of Orders

**Section 17(1) - Application for variation**

A court may make a variation order that varies, rescinds or suspends, prospectively or retroactively:
- A parenting order
- A child support order
- A spousal support order

**Section 17(5) - Change of circumstances for parenting order**

Before the court makes a variation order in respect of a parenting order, the court must be satisfied that there has been a change in the circumstances of the child since the making of the parenting order.

**What is a "material change in circumstances"?**

The change must be:
1. **Material** - Significant, not trivial
2. **Relevant** - Affects the child or parenting arrangement
3. **Unanticipated** - Not contemplated when original order made
4. **Ongoing** - Not temporary

**Examples of material changes:**

✅ **Likely material changes:**
- Relocation of parent affecting parenting time
- Significant change in child's needs (health, development, school)
- Change in parent's ability to care for child (health, work schedule, new relationship)
- Family violence incidents
- Substance abuse issues
- Serious parenting concerns
- Child's expressed preferences (depending on age/maturity)

❌ **Likely NOT material changes:**
- Normal developmental changes
- Temporary work schedule change
- New romantic relationship (unless affects child)
- Minor disagreements about parenting
- Financial changes (for parenting orders)

**Section 17(6.1) - Best interests standard for variation**

In making a variation order that varies a parenting order, the court shall apply the best interests of the child test (Section 16).

**Section 17(8) - Variation of child support**

For child support variation, the court must be satisfied that:
- A change in the circumstances has occurred since the order was made, AND
- The variation is in the best interests of the child

**Material change for child support:**
- Change in income (increase or decrease)
- Change in parenting arrangement (e.g., becomes shared parenting)
- Change in special expenses (Section 7)
- Change in number of children supported

## 💰 Section 16.4 - Parenting Plan

**Section 16.4 - Parenting plan may be filed**

A person may file with the court a parenting plan that:
- Sets out parenting time
- Sets out decision-making responsibility
- May address any other matters

**Parenting plan is NOT binding:**
- Court is not bound by the plan
- Court still applies best interests test
- Useful evidence of parents' intentions

---

## 🧒 Section 16.5 - Parenting Order - Non-parent

**Section 16.5**

The court may make a parenting order with respect to a person other than a spouse on application by that person if:
- The court considers the person to have a parent-like relationship with the child
- The court is satisfied it would be in the best interests of the child

**Examples:**
- Grandparent who has been primary caregiver
- Step-parent who has acted in parental role
- Other relative with significant caregiving role

**Test for "parent-like relationship":**
- Resided with the child
- Shared parenting responsibilities
- Contributed to child's financial support
- Represented themselves as a parent
- Emotional ties with the child

## 📅 Section 16.9 - Relocation

**Section 16.9(1) - Notice of relocation**

A person who intends to relocate must give notice to:
- Each person who has parenting time, decision-making responsibility, or a pending proceeding
- Any other person specified by court order

**Section 16.9(2) - Form and content of notice**

Notice must be in writing and include:
- Date relocation will occur (at least 60 days' notice)
- Address of new place of residence
- Contact information
- Proposal for parenting time/decision-making responsibility

**Section 16.9(3) - When notice not required**

Notice not required if:
- Order or written agreement says otherwise
- No impact on ability to exercise parenting time/decision-making responsibility
- Reasonable efforts to provide notice impossible

**Section 16.9(6) - Objection**

Person receiving notice may object by filing with court within 30 days after notice.

**Section 16.9(7) - No objection filed**

If no objection within 30 days, person may relocate with child in accordance with notice.

**Section 16.9(8) - Objection filed**

If objection filed, person must not relocate unless:
- Court authorizes relocation, OR
- Every person who could object agrees

**Court considerations for relocation:**

Under Section 16.92, court considers:
- Reasons for relocation
- Impact on child
- Amount of time spent with each person
- Existing parenting arrangements
- Relationship between child and family members
- Degree to which each person has fulfilled parenting obligations
- Degree to which parenting time can be exercised if relocation occurs
- Child's views and preferences
- Reasonableness of proposal for parenting time after relocation

---

## 🔍 Section 16.7 - Appointment to Assist

**Section 16.7 - Court may appoint**

The court may appoint a person to:
- Assist in resolving any matter relating to a parenting order
- Prepare a report on any matter
- Assess any matter (needs and circumstances of child)

**Types of appointments:**
- **Mediator** - Help parents reach agreement
- **Parenting coordinator** - Ongoing dispute resolution
- **Assessor** - Evaluate and report on best interests
- **Voice of the child professional** - Report child's views

## ⚖️ Practical Application

### Common Questions

**Q: Does "maximum contact" mean 50/50 time?**
A: No. It means as much time as serves the child's best interests - could be equal, could be primary-access, could be any arrangement.

**Q: Can past relationship problems affect custody?**
A: Only if relevant to parenting ability or involved family violence. General relationship conflicts don't matter.

**Q: What if my ex won't cooperate on decisions?**
A: Courts can allocate sole decision-making responsibility when parents can't cooperate. Document communication attempts and conflicts.

**Q: Can I move with my child?**
A: Must give 60 days' notice. Other parent can object within 30 days. If objection filed, need court permission or agreement.

**Q: What is family violence under the Act?**
A: Very broad definition - includes psychological abuse, financial control, coercive control, animal harm, and exposure of child to violence.
