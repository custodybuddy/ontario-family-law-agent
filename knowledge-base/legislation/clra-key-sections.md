# Children's Law Reform Act (CLRA) - Key Sections for Ontario Family Law

**Last Updated:** April 19, 2026  
**Jurisdiction:** Ontario  
**Statute:** Children's Law Reform Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. C.12  
**Current Version:** As amended

---

## 📋 Overview

The Children's Law Reform Act (CLRA) is Ontario provincial legislation that governs custody, access, and guardianship of children. It applies to:

- **Unmarried parents** (common-law or never married)
- **Married parents** who are separated but not divorced
- **Grandparents and other relatives** seeking access
- **Non-parent custody** situations

**Key Distinction from Divorce Act:**
- **Divorce Act:** Applies to married/divorcing parents (federal law)
- **CLRA:** Applies to unmarried parents and others (Ontario law)
- **Both:** Use "best interests of the child" test

**Note:** The CLRA still uses the terms "custody" and "access" (unlike the Divorce Act which changed to "parenting time" and "decision-making responsibility" in 2021).

---

## 🎯 Core Sections

### **Part I - Paternity**

**Section 4 - Presumption of Paternity**

A man is presumed to be the father if:
- He is married to the mother at the time of birth
- He was married to the mother and she gives birth within 300 days of marriage ending
- He marries the mother after birth and acknowledges paternity
- He acknowledges paternity in writing

**Section 7 - Declaration of Parentage**

Court can make declaration that person is or is not a parent of a child.

**Why this matters:** Establishes legal rights and obligations. Without established paternity, father has no automatic custody/access rights.

---

### **Part III - Custody, Access, and Guardianship**

**Section 18 - Definitions**

**"Custody"** includes care, upbringing, and any incident of custody.

**"Access"** includes the right to visit with and be visited by the child and the same right as a parent to make inquiries and to be given information regarding the health, education and welfare of the child.

**Important:** Access is NOT just "visiting" - it includes information rights even if child doesn't physically visit.

---

**Section 19 - Application for Custody or Access**

**(1) Who can apply:**
- A parent
- Any other person (with leave of court)

**(2) Right to notice:**
Parents entitled to notice of application by non-parent.

**Practical meaning:** Grandparents, step-parents, or other caregivers CAN apply for custody or access, but must get court permission first.

---

**Section 20 - Equal Parental Rights**

**(1) Equal entitlement:**
The father and mother of a child are equally entitled to custody of the child.

**(2) Equal right to access:**
A person entitled to access to a child has the same right as a person entitled to custody to make inquiries and to be given information regarding the health, education and welfare of the child.

**Critical:** Both parents have EQUAL rights at law, regardless of gender. Courts start from position of equality.

---

**Section 21 - Access**

**(1) Right to access:**
A parent of a child who is not entitled to custody is entitled to access to the child unless the court otherwise orders.

**(2) Access by others:**
Court may grant right of access to a child to any person.

**Why this matters:** 
- Non-custodial parent has presumptive right to access
- Court can order access for grandparents, step-parents, etc.
- Access can only be denied if not in child's best interests

---

**Section 22 - Order Restraining Harassment**

Court may make interim or final order restraining a party from:
- Molesting
- Annoying  
- Harassing
- Communicating with or contacting

the applicant or children in the applicant's lawful custody.

**Practical application:** Can get restraining order as part of custody proceeding. Protects from harassment during court process.

---

**Section 23 - Custody and Access - Separation Agreements**

**(1) Effect of agreement:**
A separation agreement may validly provide for custody or access.

**(2) Court authority:**
The court may disregard a separation agreement if not in best interests of the child.

**Key point:** Parents can agree to custody/access, but court always has authority to override if not in child's best interests. Cannot contract away child's rights.

---

**Section 24 - Best Interests of Child**

**(1) Paramount consideration:**
The merits of an application under this Part in respect of custody of or access to a child shall be determined on the basis of the best interests of the child, in accordance with subsection (2).

**(2) Best interests factors:**

The court shall consider all the child's needs and circumstances, including:

**a) Love, affection and emotional ties**
Between child and:
- Each person entitled to or claiming custody or access
- Other members of child's family who reside with the child
- Persons involved in the child's care and upbringing

**b) Child's views and preferences**
Taking into account the child's age and maturity.

**c) Length of time in stable home**
The length of time the child has lived in a stable home environment.

**d) Ability and willingness to provide guidance**
Each person's ability and willingness to provide the child with guidance, education, necessities of life and any special needs.

**e) Plans for the child's care**
Any plans proposed for the child's care and upbringing.

**f) Permanence and stability**
The permanence and stability of the family unit with which it is proposed that the child will live.

**g) Relationship by blood or adoption**
The relationship by blood or through an adoption order between the child and each person who is a party to the application.

**h) Family violence**
The effect of any family violence, including:
- Whether the child has been directly or indirectly exposed to family violence
- The harm or risk of harm to the child's physical, psychological and emotional safety, security and well-being
- The ability and willingness of the person engaging in family violence to care for and meet the needs of the child

**Practical Guidance:**

**Most important factors:**
- Child's physical, emotional, and psychological needs
- Quality of relationships
- Stability and continuity
- Each parent's ability to meet child's needs

**Less important:**
- Parent's marital status
- Parent's financial resources (unless affects basic needs)
- Parent's religion (unless affects child's wellbeing)
- Parent's gender

**Courts do NOT:**
- Automatically favor mothers
- Automatically favor biological parents over others
- Prefer one family structure over another
- Punish parents for relationship breakdown

---

**Section 24.1 - Past Conduct**

**(1) General rule:**
The past conduct of a person is not relevant to a determination of an application in respect of custody of or access to a child unless the conduct is relevant to the ability of the person to act as a parent.

**(2) Exception for family violence:**
Family violence is always relevant to ability to act as a parent.

**What this means:**
- Old affairs, lifestyle choices, past mistakes generally NOT relevant
- Focus is on current parenting ability
- EXCEPTION: Violence, abuse, neglect always relevant

---

**Section 25 - Declining Jurisdiction**

Court may decline jurisdiction if it appears that another jurisdiction is more appropriate.

**Practical:** If parents live in different provinces, court considers:
- Where child habitually resides
- Where parents reside
- Where evidence located
- Financial hardship
- Child's connections to each jurisdiction

---

**Section 28 - Variation Orders**

**(1) Court may vary:**
On application, court may vary, rescind or suspend a custody or access order.

**(2) Material change required:**
Court may make order only if there has been a material change in circumstances.

**Material change test:**
- Change must be significant (not minor)
- Change must affect the child (not just parent's convenience)
- Change was not contemplated in original order
- Change makes variation in child's best interests

**Examples of material change:**
- ✅ Parent relocating to different city
- ✅ Child's needs significantly changed (medical, educational)
- ✅ Pattern of access violations
- ✅ New evidence of safety concerns
- ✅ Parent's life circumstances substantially changed

**NOT material change:**
- ❌ Parent's new relationship (unless affects child)
- ❌ Parent's improved finances (unless was barrier to parenting)
- ❌ Child gets older (normal development)
- ❌ Parent simply wants different arrangement

---

**Section 30 - Assessment**

**(1) Order for assessment:**
Court may, on motion, appoint a person to assess and report on the needs of the child and the ability and willingness of the parties to satisfy those needs.

**(2) Scope of assessment:**
Can include:
- Interviews with child, parents, others
- Home visits
- Psychological testing
- Review of records
- Consultation with professionals

**Types of assessments:**

**Voice of the Child Report (s.30 assessment):**
- Focuses on child's views and wishes
- Shorter, less invasive
- Lower cost (~$3,000-$8,000)
- Used when child's views are key issue

**Full Custody/Access Assessment:**
- Comprehensive evaluation
- Psychological testing, home visits
- Higher cost (~$10,000-$30,000)
- Used in complex or high-conflict cases

**Office of the Children's Lawyer (OCL):**
- Government-funded
- Must meet criteria (high conflict, complex issues)
- No cost to parents
- Can include clinical investigation and/or legal representation

**Cost:** Court can order how assessment costs are divided.

---

**Section 34 - Enforcement of Custody Orders**

**(1) Power to locate child:**
Court may authorize person to locate, apprehend, and deliver child to person entitled to custody.

**(2) Warrant to apprehend:**
Court may issue warrant to bring child before court.

**When used:** If one parent refuses to return child or violates custody order.

---

**Section 35 - Order for Non-Parent Custody**

**(1) Court may order:**
Court may grant custody to person other than parent if in child's best interests.

**(2) Factors:**
Same best interests test applies.

**Common scenarios:**
- Grandparents raising grandchildren
- Child has lived with relative for extended period
- Parents unable to care for child
- Child in kinship care arrangement

**Threshold:** Must show parents unable or unwilling to properly care for child, AND non-parent custody in best interests.

---

**Section 36 - Effect of Divorce Proceeding**

Application under CLRA stays (pauses) if divorce proceeding commenced for same custody/access issue.

**Practical:** Can't have both CLRA and Divorce Act applications ongoing for same child. Divorce Act takes precedence.

## 🎯 Key Practical Points

### **Custody vs. Access**

**Custody means:**
- Physical care and control of child
- Day-to-day decision-making
- Legal right to make major decisions (education, health, religion)
- Right to choose child's residence

**Access means:**
- Time spent with child (visits)
- Right to information about child
- Right to participate in major decisions (if specified)
- Does NOT include right to change child's residence

### **Joint Custody vs. Sole Custody**

**Joint Custody:**
- Both parents share decision-making
- Requires ability to communicate and cooperate
- Can have various living arrangements (equal time, primary residence)
- Both parents involved in major decisions

**Sole Custody:**
- One parent has decision-making authority
- Other parent usually has access
- Appropriate when high conflict, safety concerns, or inability to cooperate
- Does NOT mean other parent has no rights (still has access and information rights)

### **Supervised Access**

Court may order access to be supervised if:
- Safety concerns about child
- Parent-child relationship needs rebuilding
- History of abuse or violence
- Substance abuse issues
- Mental health concerns

**Supervision can be:**
- At supervised access center
- By family member agreed upon
- By professional supervisor (paid)

### **Access Denial**

Court can deny or limit access if:
- Parent poses risk to child's safety
- Access would harm child's wellbeing
- Parent has history of abuse/violence
- Parent unable to care for child during access

**Test:** Access presumed to be in child's best interests unless evidence shows otherwise.

## 📊 How CLRA and Divorce Act Interact

| Factor | CLRA | Divorce Act |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **Who it applies to** | Unmarried parents, grandparents, others | Married/divorcing parents |
| **Terminology** | Custody, Access | Parenting time, Decision-making responsibility |
| **Best interests test** | Section 24 | Section 16 |
| **Jurisdiction** | Ontario courts | Federal (applied in Ontario) |
| **When both could apply** | If parents married | Divorce Act takes precedence |

**Key:** If parents are separating but married, they can use CLRA initially, but once divorce proceedings start, must use Divorce Act framework.

## 🚨 Common Misconceptions

**MYTH:** "Mothers automatically get custody"
**REALITY:** Both parents have equal rights under s.20. Courts decide based solely on best interests, not gender.

**MYTH:** "Biological parents always have priority"
**REALITY:** Court can grant custody to non-parent if in child's best interests (s.35).

**MYTH:** "Access is just visiting time"
**REALITY:** Access includes right to information and to participate in child's life, even without physical visits (s.21(2)).

**MYTH:** "Child can decide at age 12"
**REALITY:** There's no "age of choice". Child's views considered based on maturity, but court decides based on best interests.

**MYTH:** "Joint custody means equal time"
**REALITY:** Joint custody is about decision-making. Time division is separate and can be any arrangement.

**MYTH:** "If I have sole custody, the other parent has no say"
**REALITY:** Even with sole custody, other parent retains access and information rights unless court orders otherwise.

## 📚 Case Law Principles

**Leading Cases Under CLRA:**

**Catholic Children's Aid Society v. M. (C.)** (1994)
- Established that best interests is paramount
- Cultural/religious factors are one consideration among many

**Office of the Children's Lawyer v. Balev** (2018)
- Child's views are important but not determinative
- Court must weigh all best interests factors

**Smith v. Smith** (1988)
- Material change required for variation
- Change must be significant and unanticipated

**Strobridge v. Strobridge** (1994)
- Maximum contact principle
- Child benefits from relationship with both parents (unless safety concerns)

## 💡 Practice Tips

**For Self-Represented Parents:**

1. **Document everything** - Keep records of parenting, communication, involvement
2. **Focus on the child** - Court cares about best interests, not parent's grievances
3. **Be specific** - Proposed custody/access schedule should be detailed and workable
4. **Show willingness to cooperate** - Courts favor parents who facilitate child's relationship with other parent
5. **Address concerns properly** - Safety concerns need evidence, not allegations
6. **Consider s.30 assessment** - If high conflict or child's views unclear
7. **Parallel parenting** - Option when can't cooperate but both involved

**Red Flags to Avoid:**

- ❌ Blocking access without court order (can lose custody)
- ❌ Speaking negatively about other parent to child (alienation)
- ❌ Using child as messenger or spy
- ❌ Making major decisions unilaterally without authority
- ❌ Not following court order (contempt)
- ❌ Introducing new partners to child without proper transition
- ❌ Withholding information from other parent
