# Child Support Guidelines - Key Sections for Ontario Family Law

**Last Updated:** April 19, 2026  
**Jurisdiction:** Federal (Applies in Ontario)  
**Statute:** Federal Child Support Guidelines, SOR/97-175  
**Current Version:** 2025 Federal Tables (Effective October 1, 2025)

---

## 🚨 IMPORTANT: October 2025 Table Updates

**The Federal Child Support Tables were updated on October 1, 2025** — the first comprehensive revision since 2017. This affects all new child support calculations and existing orders.

**Key Changes:**

✅ **New Income Floor:** Minimum income to owe support increased from $13,000 to $16,000
- Parents earning under $16,000 now have $0 table obligation
- Due to federal basic personal amount increase (from $11,424 in 2017 to $15,000 in 2024)

✅ **Lower Income Ranges ($16,000-$45,000):** Generally **decreased** amounts compared to 2017 tables
- Significant reductions for payors in this income bracket
- Reflects updated tax rules and personal exemptions

✅ **Higher Income Ranges ($45,000+):** Small changes (typically 1-2% increase/decrease)
- Minimal impact for most middle/upper-income payors
- One or two children: usually under 2% difference from 2017

**Impact on Existing Orders:**

❌ **Orders made BEFORE October 1, 2025 do NOT automatically update**
- Your existing court order or agreement stays at the original amount
- You must apply to vary (change) the order to use new table amounts
- The table update itself can be a "material change in circumstances"

✅ **New orders/agreements AFTER October 1, 2025 must use 2025 tables**
- All new calculations use updated amounts
- Cannot use 2017 tables for post-October 1, 2025 agreements

**Historical Table Reference:**
- For support owed **November 22, 2017 - September 30, 2025**: Use 2017 Tables
- For support owed **October 1, 2025 onward**: Use 2025 Tables

## 📋 Overview

The Federal Child Support Guidelines set out the rules for calculating child support across Canada, including Ontario. The goal is to:

- Establish fair and consistent child support amounts
- Reduce conflict and tension between parents
- Ensure children benefit from both parents' financial means
- Encourage settlement between parents
- Make child support predictable and consistent

**Key Principle:** Child support is the **RIGHT OF THE CHILD**, not the parent. Parents cannot waive or reduce support below guideline amounts.

## 🎯 Core Sections

### **Section 1 - Definitions**

**"Child"**
- Biological or adopted child of the parent
- Under age of majority (18 in Ontario), OR
- Over 18 but unable to obtain necessities due to illness, disability, or other cause, OR
- Over 18 but pursuing full-time education

**"Income"**
Total income as determined under sections 16-20 (Line 15000 on tax return, with adjustments)

**"Spouse"**
Either of two persons who are married to each other or are living together in a conjugal relationship.

---

### **Section 2 - Objectives**

Child support orders should:
1. Establish fair standard of support for children
2. Reduce conflict and tension
3. Improve efficiency of legal process
4. Ensure consistent treatment of spouses and children

---

### **Section 3 - Basic Amount of Child Support**

**(1) Table amount:**
Unless special provisions apply, the amount of child support is the amount from the applicable table based on:
- Number of children under 18
- Province or territory where paying parent lives
- Paying parent's income

**How to find table amount:**
1. Determine paying parent's annual income
2. Count number of children under 18 receiving support
3. Look up amount in table for paying parent's province
4. That is the base child support amount

**Tables are different for each province** because cost of living varies.

**Ontario Table Example (2026):**

| Income | 1 Child | 2 Children | 3 Children | 4+ Children |
|--------|---------|------------|------------|-------------|
| $30,000 | $256 | $453 | $585 | $693 |
| $50,000 | $442 | $726 | $928 | $1,090 |
| $75,000 | $679 | $1,116 | $1,426 | $1,675 |
| $100,000 | $891 | $1,462 | $1,868 | $2,196 |
| $150,000 | $1,308 | $2,144 | $2,740 | $3,221 |

*Actual table goes up to $150,000+ with different formulas above that*

---

### **Section 4 - Income Over $150,000**

If paying parent's income exceeds $150,000:

**(a) Table amount for first $150,000**, PLUS

**(b) Court's discretion for amount over $150,000:**
Court may order:
- The table percentage for the excess income, OR
- A different amount if table amount would be inappropriate

**Why discretion exists:** At high incomes, children's needs may not increase proportionally. Court considers child's actual needs and circumstances.

---

### **Section 5 - Spouse in Place of a Parent (Step-Parent)**

If paying parent is a step-parent (stands in place of a parent):

**(1) Court may order amount different from tables** considering:
- Child support obligations of other parent
- Step-parent's legal duty to support child
- Whether step-parent contributed to child's support

**Threshold:** Step-parent must have "stood in place of a parent" - provided financial support, held out child as own, intended to treat child as own.

---

### **Section 7 - Special or Extraordinary Expenses**

**(1) Add-ons to base support** for:

**a) Childcare expenses** (daycare, babysitting) incurred as a result of:
- Employment
- Illness/disability of custodial parent
- Education/training for employment

**b) Medical and dental insurance premiums** for the child

**c) Health-related expenses exceeding $100 annually:**
- Orthodontics, dental work
- Professional counseling/therapy
- Prescription drugs, glasses/contacts
- Medical devices/equipment

**d) Extraordinary expenses for primary/secondary education:**
- Private school tuition (if reasonable given parents' means)
- Educational programs for special needs
- Tutoring/special programs (if needed)

**e) Post-secondary education expenses:**
- Tuition, books, residence
- Transportation to/from school
- Living expenses

**f) Extraordinary extracurricular activities:**
- Must be extraordinary given parents' means
- Examples: competitive sports, elite programs, expensive lessons

**(2) Sharing of expenses:**
Expenses shared in proportion to parents' incomes, after deducting:
- Any subsidy, benefit or income tax deduction
- Any contribution from the child (student loans, scholarships)

**Formula:**
```
Parent's share = (Parent's income / Combined income) × Expense
```

**Example:**
Parent A income: $80,000
Parent B income: $40,000
Combined: $120,000

Daycare expense: $12,000/year

Parent A share: ($80,000/$120,000) × $12,000 = $8,000
Parent B share: ($40,000/$120,000) × $12,000 = $4,000

---

### **Section 8 - Split Custody**

When **each parent has at least one child** living with them:

**(1) Set-off amount:**
- Calculate each parent's table obligation for children with other parent
- Subtract lower amount from higher amount
- Higher earner pays the difference

**Example:**
Parent A (income $70,000) has 2 children
Parent B (income $50,000) has 1 child

Parent A would pay for 1 child at $70K: $634
Parent B would pay for 2 children at $50K: $726

Set-off: $726 - $634 = $92
Parent B pays Parent A $92/month

**Why:** Avoids parents exchanging money back and forth.

---

### **Section 9 - Shared Custody**

When **a child spends 40% or more time** with each parent:

**(1) Amount different from tables** determined by considering:

**a) Table amounts** for each parent

**b) Increased costs** of shared custody:
- Duplicated expenses (clothing, toys, furniture at both homes)
- Travel costs for transitions

**c) Conditions, means, needs** of each parent and child

**Why discretion:** Shared parenting creates unique financial situations that tables don't fully capture.

**Common approaches:**

**Set-off method:**
- Calculate each parent's table amount
- Subtract lower from higher
- Adjust for actual time with each parent

**Multiplier method:**
- Calculate time percentage with each parent
- Multiply table amount by that percentage
- May add shared costs

**Practical note:** Shared custody does NOT automatically mean no support. Parent with higher income usually still pays.

---

### **Section 10 - Undue Hardship**

**(1) Court may order different amount** if table amount would cause undue hardship to:
- The paying parent, OR
- The receiving parent, OR
- A child

**(2) Circumstances that may cause hardship:**

**a) Unusually high debts** reasonably incurred to:
- Support family before separation
- Earn a living

**b) Unusually high expenses** for:
- Exercising access to child
- Supporting a child under legal duty (other children)
- Supporting current spouse/common-law partner or their child
- Supporting other person under legal duty

**c) Legal duty** to support:
- Other children
- Current spouse/common-law partner
- Person from another relationship

**(3) Standards of living test:**
Court can only reduce support for undue hardship if the household claiming hardship has a **LOWER standard of living** than other household.

**Household includes:**
- Parent
- Any new partner
- Any children in the home
- Any dependents

**Why this test exists:** Prevents high-income parent from reducing support while maintaining higher standard of living.

---

### **Sections 15-20 - Income Determination**

### **Section 16 - Calculation of Annual Income**

**(1) Start with Line 15000** of T1 General tax return (Total Income)

**(2) Adjust using Schedule III** (see below)

### **Section 17 - Pattern of Income**

If income varied significantly over last 3 years, court may use average or longer period.

### **Section 18 - Shareholder/Controlling Interest**

If parent controls a corporation:
- Include pre-tax corporate income
- Include benefits received from corporation
- Adjust for reasonableness

### **Section 19 - Imputing Income**

Court may impute (assign) income higher than actual if parent:

**a) Intentionally under-employed or unemployed:**
- Capable of earning more but chooses not to
- EXCEPT if attending school or unable due to health/child care

**b) Exempt from income tax:**
- Diplomatic immunity
- Income from tax-free jurisdiction

**c) Lives in country with lower income tax rates**

**d) Non-disclosure or incomplete disclosure:**
- Refuses to provide financial information
- Provides incomplete or misleading info

**e) Unreasonably deducts expenses** from business

**f) Derives income from property:**
- Property not reasonably used to generate income
- Could generate more income if used differently

**g) Has diverted income:**
- Routes income through corporation or trust
- Pays excessive salary to new partner
- Lives beyond reported means

**h) Obtains non-taxable benefits:**
- Through corporation or trust
- Company car, housing, etc.

**How income is imputed:**
- Earning capacity (what parent could earn)
- Similar employment in the area
- Education, training, experience
- Past earnings
- Any relevant factors

**Example:** Lawyer claims to earn $30,000 but historically earned $150,000 - court may impute $150,000.

---

### **Section 20 - Reasonable Expenses**

For self-employed parents:

**(1) Deduct only reasonable expenses** necessary to earn income

**(2) Cannot deduct:**
- Capital cost allowances for:
  - Real property (rental depreciation)
  - Vehicle if claimed actual vehicle expenses
- Personal portion of expenses
- Non-income-earning activities

**Why:** Prevents self-employed parents from artificially reducing income through aggressive tax deductions.

---

## 📊 Schedule III - Adjustments to Income

**Add back:**
- Capital gains/losses (actual amounts, not 50%)
- Business investment losses
- Carrying charges exceeding investment income
- Employment expenses claimed
- Stock option deductions

**Deduct:**
- Union/professional dues
- Child support paid for other children
- Spousal support paid

**Goal:** Get to actual income available for child support.

## 💰 Practical Examples

### **Example 1: Basic Table Amount**

**Scenario:**
- Parent earns $65,000/year in Ontario
- 2 children under 18
- No special expenses

**Calculation:**
Ontario table at $65,000 for 2 children = **$926/month**

---

### **Example 2: Section 7 Expenses**

**Scenario:**
Parent A income: $90,000
Parent B income: $60,000
2 children

Expenses:
- Daycare: $1,200/month = $14,400/year
- Orthodontics: $4,000/year
- Hockey program: $3,000/year (extraordinary)

**Calculation:**
Combined income: $150,000
Parent A percentage: 90/150 = 60%
Parent B percentage: 60/150 = 40%

Base support (Parent A pays Parent B):
Ontario table at $90K for 2 children = $1,462/month

Section 7 expenses (annual):
Total: $14,400 + $4,000 + $3,000 = $21,400

Parent A pays: 60% × $21,400 = $12,840/year = $1,070/month
Parent B pays: 40% × $21,400 = $8,560/year = $713/month

**Total Parent A pays: $1,462 + $1,070 = $2,532/month**

---

### **Example 3: Shared Custody (Set-off)**

**Scenario:**
Children spend 60% time with Parent A, 40% with Parent B
Parent A income: $55,000
Parent B income: $80,000
2 children

**Calculation:**

If 100% with Parent A:
Parent B pays: Ontario table at $80K for 2 children = $1,294/month

If 100% with Parent B:
Parent A pays: Ontario table at $55K for 2 children = $815/month

Set-off: $1,294 - $815 = $479

Adjust for time split (40/60):
Many courts use: $479 × (percentage adjustment)

**Final: Parent B pays approximately $300-400/month**
*(Exact amount depends on court's shared custody formula and actual costs)*

## 🚨 Common Issues & Misconceptions

**MYTH:** "If we have 50/50 custody, no one pays support"
**REALITY:** Time-sharing doesn't eliminate support. Parent with higher income usually still pays (Section 9).

**MYTH:** "I can reduce support if I lose my job"
**REALITY:** Depends on why. Voluntary unemployment may result in imputed income (Section 19).

**MYTH:** "Child support ends at 18"
**REALITY:** Continues if child in full-time school, disabled, or unable to support self.

**MYTH:** "I can pay less if I have a new baby"
**REALITY:** New family obligations MAY be undue hardship (Section 10), but must prove lower standard of living.

**MYTH:** "Child support covers everything"
**REALITY:** Table amount is basic support. Section 7 expenses (daycare, medical, extracurriculars) are extra.

**MYTH:** "My ex makes more now, so my support should be less"
**REALITY:** Recipient's income doesn't reduce paying parent's obligation (except in shared custody).

**MYTH:** "I don't have to pay if I don't see my kids"
**REALITY:** Support and access are separate. Support is child's right.

**MYTH:** "The October 2025 table update automatically changes my existing order"
**REALITY:** Existing orders stay the same until someone applies to vary. The update does NOT automatically adjust your payment.

## 📅 October 2025 Table Changes - Practical Implications

### **Who Should Review Their Order?**

**✅ Payors earning $16,000-$45,000:**
- Likely paying LESS under 2025 tables than 2017
- Significant potential for reduction
- Worth calculating to see difference

**✅ Recipients whose payor's income has increased since original order:**
- Good time to bring variation application
- Can address BOTH table update AND income increase
- More efficient than separate applications

**✅ Anyone with order from 2017-2024:**
- Tables have changed since your order was made
- Check if new amount differs meaningfully
- Consider variation if difference is substantial

**❌ Don't bother if:**
- Order is recent (made in 2025 after October 1)
- Difference is minimal (under $25/month)
- Income hasn't changed significantly
- Near end of support obligation

### **How Much Will It Change?**

**Income $16,000 or less:**
- **2017 tables:** Had minimum payment
- **2025 tables:** $0 obligation
- **Reduction:** 100%

**Income $16,000-$25,000:**
- **Typical reduction:** $50-$150/month depending on children
- **Example:** $20K income, 1 child
  - 2017: ~$120/month
  - 2025: ~$80/month
  - Difference: $40/month ($480/year)

**Income $25,000-$45,000:**
- **Typical change:** $20-$80/month reduction
- **Example:** $35K income, 2 children
  - 2017: ~$450/month
  - 2025: ~$400/month
  - Difference: $50/month ($600/year)

**Income $45,000-$80,000:**
- **Typical change:** 1-2% (±$10-$30/month)
- **Example:** $60K income, 2 children
  - 2017: ~$892/month
  - 2025: ~$910/month
  - Difference: ~$18/month ($216/year)

**Income $80,000+:**
- **Typical change:** Under 2%
- May increase or decrease slightly
- Generally not worth variation application for table change alone

### **Variation Application Process**

**1. Calculate the difference:**
- Use 2025 table lookup tool
- Compare to current order amount
- Determine if "material change"

**2. Material change threshold:**
- No fixed dollar amount
- Courts look at:
  - Percentage change (>10% more likely to qualify)
  - Impact on paying parent
  - Impact on receiving parent
  - Time remaining on support obligation
- **Example:** $50/month change over 8 years remaining = $4,800 total (likely material)

**3. File motion to vary:**
- Form 15: Motion to Change
- Updated financial disclosure (Form 13/13.1)
- Affidavit explaining change
- Serve on other parent

**4. Timeline:**
- Other parent has 30 days to respond
- May resolve on consent
- Otherwise case conference → settlement conference
- Trial if no agreement

**5. Effective date:**
- Usually effective from date of filing (not retroactive to October 1)
- Court has discretion
- May be retroactive if delay was reasonable

### **Consent vs. Court Application**

**Best case: Consent**
- Calculate new amount together
- Sign Form 15 consent
- File with court
- Faster, cheaper, less conflict

**If no consent:**
- File motion on your own
- Other parent can oppose
- Court decides based on evidence
- Legal fees, court time required

### **Combining with Other Changes**

**Smart strategy:**
If you're varying for table update, also address:
- Income changes since original order
- Changed parenting time percentage
- New Section 7 expenses
- Child aging out of daycare/activities

**One application is more efficient than multiple.**

### **Don't Wait Too Long**

**Time limits:**
- No limitation period for variation
- BUT delay may affect retroactive claims
- Earlier you apply, earlier change takes effect

**Example of delay impact:**
- October 1, 2025: Tables change
- April 19, 2026: You apply to vary (TODAY)
- Order effective April 19, 2026
- You've paid 6+ months at old rate
- Court unlikely to make retroactive to October 1

### **When NOT to Vary**

**Don't apply if:**
- ❌ Difference is minimal (under $20/month)
- ❌ Support obligation ending soon (under 1 year left)
- ❌ Other parent will fight and legal fees exceed savings
- ❌ Your income has increased enough to offset table decrease
- ❌ You have arrears (payor) - focus on compliance first

### **October 2025 Changes: Action Checklist**

**For Payors:**
- [ ] Use 2025 table calculator with your current income
- [ ] Compare to current order amount
- [ ] Calculate annual difference (monthly × 12 × years remaining)
- [ ] If substantial, discuss consent variation with recipient
- [ ] If no consent, consult lawyer about motion to change
- [ ] Update income disclosure (last 3 years)
- [ ] File within reasonable time to maximize effective date

**For Recipients:**
- [ ] Check if payor's income has changed since original order
- [ ] Use 2025 tables with payor's CURRENT income (not original)
- [ ] If income increased, variation addresses both issues
- [ ] If payor applies to reduce, verify their income hasn't risen
- [ ] Request updated financial disclosure
- [ ] Consider Section 7 expense changes too
- [ ] Respond within 30 days to any motion filed

## 📝 Income Disclosure Requirements

**Paying parent must provide:**

- Last 3 years' tax returns (T1 General)
- Last 3 years' Notices of Assessment
- Last 3 years' T4s, T4As, T5s
- If self-employed:
  - Financial statements for last 3 years
  - Statement of income and expenses (Form 13.1)
- If controls corporation:
  - Corporate tax returns
  - Financial statements
- Any other relevant income documents

**Receiving parent must provide (for Section 7):**
- Income information to calculate proportionate sharing
- Receipts for claimed Section 7 expenses

**Timeline:**
- 30 days from request or court order
- Failure to disclose can result in imputed income

## 🔧 Variation of Child Support

**When can support be changed?**

Under Divorce Act s.17 or CLRA s.37:
- Material change in circumstances

**What counts as material change:**
- ✅ Significant income change (loss of job, promotion, new career)
- ✅ Child's needs changed (special education, medical needs)
- ✅ Custody arrangement changed
- ✅ Child reached age of majority but still dependent
- ✅ Paying parent has new child (may be undue hardship)

**What does NOT count:**
- ❌ Receiving parent's income increased (doesn't change paying parent's obligation)
- ❌ Paying parent voluntarily quit job (may result in imputed income)
- ❌ Normal cost of living increases
- ❌ Paying parent "can't afford it" without proof

## 🚨 Enforcement

**Family Responsibility Office (FRO):**

If court orders child support in Ontario, enforcement usually through FRO:

**FRO can:**
- Garnish wages
- Garnish bank accounts
- Intercept tax refunds
- Suspend driver's license
- Report to credit bureaus
- Register liens on property
- Deny passport renewal (for arrears over $3,000)
- Federal license denial (aviation, marine)

**Parents can opt out** of FRO if:
- Both agree in writing
- Court approves

**Risks of opting out:**
- If payor misses payment, must go back to court
- Harder to enforce
- May be ordered back to FRO

## 💡 Practice Tips

**For Self-Represented Parents:**

**If you're paying support:**
1. **Disclose fully** - Don't hide income; penalties are severe
2. **Pay through FRO** - Creates payment record
3. **Document income changes** - If income drops, apply to vary immediately
4. **Keep receipts** - For Section 7 expense sharing
5. **Don't reduce payments unilaterally** - Must get court order to change

**If you're receiving support:**
1. **Enforce through FRO** - Don't rely on goodwill
2. **Update when income changes** - You can apply to increase if their income increases
3. **Keep expense receipts** - For Section 7 claims
4. **Know the table amounts** - Verify calculations are correct
5. **Separate from access issues** - Don't withhold parenting time over money

**For both:**
- Child support is not taxable to recipient or deductible by payor (changed in 1997)
- Support is separate from parenting time
- Get legal advice for complex situations (corporations, trusts, international)
